What is TF-CBT?

Trauma-Focused Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TF-CBT) is an evidence-informed treatment for children ages 3–18 and their caregivers. Developed by Cohen, Mannarino, and Deblinger, it uses the PRACTICE framework to build coping skills, process trauma memories, and restore healthy caregiver relationships — typically delivered in 12–25 sessions.

What Is TF-CBT? (Video Guide for Parents & Caregivers)

A short, plain-language overview of what TF-CBT is, the evidence behind it, and what to expect — for parents, caregivers, and clinicians.

Independently rated

Rated a “Well-Supported, Effective Practice” — the highest scientific rating — by the California Evidence-Based Clearinghouse.

Research base

Tested in 25+ randomized controlled trials, with improvement in PTSD symptoms in 8–25 sessions, per the NCTSN.

Developed by

Created by Cohen, Mannarino & Deblinger. This hub is a curated index — not a substitute for the model owners at tfcbt.org.

Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What does PRACTICE stand for in TF-CBT?
P — Psychoeducation  |  R — Relaxation  |  A — Affective Modulation  |  C — Cognitive Coping  |  T — Trauma Narrative  |  I — In Vivo Mastery  |  C — Conjoint Sessions  |  E — Enhancing Safety.
How long does TF-CBT take?
TF-CBT typically takes 12–25 sessions over 3–6 months, delivered weekly. Each session is usually 60–90 minutes, split between individual child work, individual caregiver work, and conjoint sessions near the end of treatment.
What age range is TF-CBT designed for?
TF-CBT is designed for children and adolescents ages 3–18. Developmentally adapted protocols exist for very young children (ages 3–6), and group delivery formats exist for school and agency settings. The model always includes a caregiver as an active treatment participant.
Is TF-CBT only for sexual abuse?
No. TF-CBT was originally developed for children who experienced sexual abuse, but is now widely used for all types of childhood trauma — physical abuse, neglect, grief, domestic violence, natural disasters, accidents, and community violence. Adaptations also exist for complex and chronic trauma.
Where can I find a TF-CBT therapist?
The NCTSN treatment locator and the Psychology Today therapist finder (filter by TF-CBT) are the two best starting points. Ask a potential therapist directly whether they have completed TF-CBT training through tfcbt.org.
